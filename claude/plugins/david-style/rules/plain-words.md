# Golden Rules of Plain Writing

*After Sir Ernest Gowers — The Complete Plain Words (1954)*

---

Originally written for the British Civil Service, *The Complete Plain Words* remains the definitive guide to clear, honest, and direct English prose. These rules distil its essential guidance.

---

## The First Rule — Be clear about your purpose before you write

Before you put a word on the page, be sure you know what you are trying to say and to whom. A writer who is vague in mind will be vague on the page. Clarity of expression follows clarity of thought — it cannot substitute for it.

---

## Rule II — Use short words in preference to long ones

Anglo-Saxon words are usually shorter, plainer, and more forceful than their Latinate equivalents. Prefer the concrete, the direct, the everyday.

> Prefer: "use" not "utilise" · "find" not "ascertain" · "help" not "facilitate"

---

## Rule III — Use no more words than the meaning requires

Padding adds length but removes force. Every word should earn its place. Where two words will serve, delete three. The verb is the engine of the sentence; make it do the work.

> Prefer: "now" not "at this point in time" · "because" not "due to the fact that"

---

## Rule IV — Prefer the active voice to the passive

The active voice is more direct, more vigorous, and shorter. The passive encourages vagueness — especially about who is responsible for an action. Use it sparingly and with purpose.

> Prefer: "We have decided" not "It has been decided" · "You must apply" not "Application must be made"

---

## Rule V — Avoid abstract nouns turned from verbs

Nouns made from verbs — "examination" for "examine", "consideration" for "consider" — drain life from prose. Where possible, restore the verb and let the sentence breathe.

> Prefer: "consider" not "give consideration to" · "decide" not "reach a decision on"

---

## Rule VI — Cut out unnecessary qualifications

Words such as "quite", "rather", "somewhat", and "largely" weaken the statement they qualify. If a thing is true, say it plainly. If it is not, say what qualification is really meant.

> Prefer: "important" not "rather important" · "changed" not "somewhat modified"

---

## Rule VII — Avoid jargon and officialese

Technical terms have their place among specialists, but jargon used to impress rather than express is always a vice. "Officialese" — the bureaucratic habit of using pompous abstractions — obscures meaning and insults the reader.

> Prefer: "start" not "commence operations" · "send" not "transmit" · "end" not "terminate"

---

## Rule VIII — Keep sentences short and varied

Long sentences tire the reader and often hide unclear thinking. One main idea per sentence is a good rule. Vary the length for rhythm: a short sentence after several longer ones lands with particular force.

---

## Rule IX — Put the most important element where it will be noticed

The beginning and end of a sentence are positions of emphasis. The middle is where things are forgotten. Arrange words so the key idea arrives where it will have the most weight.

---

## Rule X — Match the register to the reader

Plain writing does not mean the same style for all occasions. Write for the reader in front of you — their knowledge, their relationship to you, and the purpose of the communication should govern your choice of words and tone.

---

## Rule XI — Do not be afraid of the first person

The habit of avoiding "I" and "we" in official writing has produced much tortured prose. Writing in the first person is direct, honest, and human. Impersonal constructions can encourage evasion of responsibility.

> Prefer: "I am sorry to inform you" not "It is regretted that notification must be given"

---

## Rule XII — Prefer the specific to the vague

Vague generalities satisfy no one. Whenever you can, give particulars. Concrete examples illuminate abstract principles. If you cannot be specific, ask yourself whether you really know what you mean.

---

## Rule XIII — Do not use words as mere gestures

Phrases like "having regard to all the circumstances" or "it may be said that" are verbal filler. They create an appearance of caution or thoroughness while adding nothing. Cut them.

---

## Rule XIV — Revise remorselessly

Good writing is rewriting. Read your work as a stranger would, with fresh eyes and no sympathy for what you meant to say. Cut what is redundant, clarify what is obscure, simplify what is elaborate.

---

## The Governing Principle

> **Writing is an act of courtesy towards the reader.**

Every rule above serves one purpose: to make the reader's work easy. Bad writing is not merely an aesthetic failing — it is an imposition on the reader's time and goodwill. Plain words are an act of respect.

---

*Sir Ernest Gowers, The Complete Plain Words (1954, revised ed. 1986)*
